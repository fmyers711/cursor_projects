Splunk & Cisco Capabilities — Driven by Consolidation, Cost Reduction & Modernization

For the CIO, CISO, CTO, and IT leadership

Deploying Splunk is anchored in three priorities that align with the FY2024–FY2027 ITS Strategic Plan: consolidation for efficiency (one analytics and security operations layer across agencies), cost reductions (automation, fewer redundant tools, and data-driven network and cloud decisions), and modernization (observability, cloud-smart governance, and evidence for application portfolio decisions). Cisco capabilities extend that stack for connectivity, threat intelligence, and end-to-end visibility. The sections below organize benefits, risks, and recommendations around these drivers.

Why Splunk: three strategic drivers

Splunk deployment is justified first by business and operational outcomes—not by tooling alone. Each driver reinforces the others: consolidation creates the platform for efficiency; efficiency and visibility expose cost takeout; modernization proceeds safely when telemetry and governance are shared.

1. Consolidation for efficiency

  • Unified security operations — Splunk Enterprise Security (ES) as the central SIEM and integration layer for security data across agencies, enriched with Cisco Talos intelligence.
  • Fewer silos, repeatable processes — Standardized correlation, dashboards, and response patterns replace inconsistent monitoring across 48 agencies.
  • Automation at scale — Splunk SOAR shifts staff from repetitive tasks to threat hunting and higher-value work (~70% automation target for repetitive security tasks).
  • Integrated stack alignment — Centralized logging with Cisco Secure Firewall and SD-WAN reduces fragmented tooling and supports a “Cyberfusion”-style operating model.

2. Cost reductions

  • Labor and toil — SOAR and automated correlation target lower MTTD/MTTR with fewer manual steps (e.g., firewall blocklist updates).
  • Network spend — Cisco ThousandEyes validates provider performance and SLAs; empirical data supports circuit consolidation and migration off expensive legacy connectivity without sacrificing reliability.
  • Infrastructure overhead — Integrated security and networking target ~20% reduction in hardware overhead; bandwidth optimization by identifying underutilized circuits.
  • Cloud discipline — Splunk cloud monitoring and governance visibility help control landing-zone spend and compliance costs.

3. Modernization

  • Application portfolio decisions — Splunk Observability Cloud and Golden Signals inform refactor, retire, or replace (~25% downtime reduction target during modernization).
  • Cloud-smart strategy — Combined visibility and policy (Splunk plus Cisco Umbrella) for landing zones, governance, and regulatory alignment.
  • Responsible AI — Splunk ITSI for auditability, bias indicators, and monitoring of AI-driven decisions (Goal 7).
  • Evidence-based change — Shared telemetry supports a shift from reactive IT to data-driven operations as legacy systems transition.

Risks mitigated

Splunk and the extended Cisco stack address risks that would otherwise block consolidation, cost takeout, and safe modernization:

  • Delayed threat detection and prolonged incidents — Addressed by standardized SIEM, correlation, and Talos-fed threat context (Goal 2).
  • Inconsistent or siloed security monitoring across agencies — Reduced by consolidating visibility and response patterns across the enterprise.
  • Human error and backlog from manual security tasks — Reduced through SOAR automation for repeatable playbooks.
  • Modernization-induced outages and performance regressions — Mitigated by observability and Golden Signals during application transition (Goal 3).
  • Hidden network and provider failures driving cost and downtime — Mitigated by end-to-end path visibility and SLA validation (ThousandEyes, Goal 4).
  • Fragmented security stacks and operational complexity — Partially consolidated through integrated firewall, SD-WAN, and centralized logging.
  • Cloud sprawl, blind spots, and compliance gaps — Mitigated by unified monitoring of landing zones and secure cloud gateway controls (Goal 6).
  • AI governance gaps (bias, unauthorized data use, weak auditability) — Addressed by model/data monitoring and decision audit trails (Goal 7).

Barriers overcome

These barriers explain why a deliberate Splunk-led consolidation matters—and how the three drivers reinforce each other:

  • Scale and fragmentation (48 agencies) — A common analytics and security operations layer helps align processes without replacing every local tool overnight.
  • Uncertainty in the “R’s of modernization” — Observability quantifies which legacy systems merit refactor versus retirement.
  • Cost pressure on legacy circuits and diverse connectivity / IRON integration — Empirical performance data supports moves to more agile, cost-effective options without sacrificing reliability.
  • Reactive IT culture — Shared telemetry, automation, and proactive monitoring support a shift toward data-driven operations and “Cyberfusion”-style convergence.
  • Policy and budget alignment in the cloud — Centralized visibility supports spend and compliance guardrails across agencies.

Recommendations for leadership

Sequence investments so consolidation establishes the platform, cost instruments are applied on credible telemetry, and modernization accelerates without blind spots:

  • Phase 1 — Consolidation & efficiency: Prioritize Splunk ES across consolidated agencies to establish baseline visibility and standardized detection/response for the Cyberfusion program.
  • Phase 2 — Cost reductions: Use Splunk ITSI and Cisco ThousandEyes to audit current network infrastructure and surface immediate cost-reduction opportunities through circuit consolidation and provider validation.
  • Phase 3 — Modernization: Implement Splunk ITSI and extend observability/cloud governance so responsible AI and application transition programs run on measurable, auditable foundations.

Integrating these platforms advances the three drivers together: a single analytics layer for efficiency, disciplined spend and operations, and safer modernization—supporting a shift from reactive IT to data-driven delivery that maximizes taxpayer value and protects citizen data.
