The challenge public-sector technology leaders face

State agencies, local governments, and higher education institutions generate vast volumes of machine data—security events, application logs, infrastructure metrics, research telemetry, and compliance evidence. Yet most organizations face an impossible choice:

  • Expensive indexing — Index everything in hot tiers and exhaust constrained budgets.
  • Dropping data — Delete or sample telemetry, creating blind spots and increased risk for security, operations, and AI.
  • Unmanaged external lakes — Dump data into cloud storage with no governance, discoverability, or path to analytics.

The result: more than 90% of operational data lands in low-cost storage—unstructured, inaccessible, and untapped. For SLED environments this is compounded by tight budgets, lean IT and SOC teams, fragmented agency or campus silos, and long-term compliance mandates that demand retention without proportional staffing growth.

Three-tier architecture

CDF organizes machine data into three retention and capability bands. Each tier maps to clear use cases—so technology leaders can align spend to mission priority rather than paying hot-tier prices for archive data.

Tier A is provided by core Splunk Indexes and premium applications. Tiers B and C are provided by the Machine Data Lake (MDL).

Tier A — Hot Real-time operations Products: Splunk Index, Enterprise Security, Observability Cloud Retention: 30–90 days (security example) Impact: Active detections, live investigation, monitoring, and service continuity
Tier B — Analytics Scaled analytics & ML Product: MDL Analytics Table Retention: 90–180 days Impact: Extended investigations, batch detections, ML training, agentic workflows
Tier C — Archive Long-term evidence Products: MDL Raw Tier / Long-Term Retention Retention: Long-term Impact: Compliance, audit, public records, forensic lookback, promotion on demand

Federated Search unifies all three tiers plus external lakes (Amazon S3, Amazon Security Lake, and expanding sources)—enabling search-in-place without replatforming fragmented agency or campus data estates.

Implications for AI and enterprise data strategy

State AI initiatives, campus research programs, and governed enterprise AI all require machine data in consumable formats—not months of ETL. CDF and MDL deliver AI-ready outputs from landed and promoted tiers; the challenge for leadership is ensuring models and agents reason over governed context, not raw telemetry firehoses.

Why raw machine data is costly for AI

Frontier AI models are powerful reasoners, but raw Metrics, Events, Logs, and Traces (MELT) are too voluminous, repetitive, context-poor, and semantically inconsistent to feed directly into agents at enterprise scale—driving prohibitive token cost, latency, and hallucination risk.

Structural challenge Impact on AI programs
Massive volume Billions of events/day exceed context windows; agents iterate in costly loops
Repetitive structure Most tokens repeat boilerplate fields—timestamps, hostnames, JSON keys—not mission signal
Context-poor at event level Single events lack causal or business meaning; models must reconstruct correlation
Semantically inconsistent Vendor-specific schemas force re-learning per source in every prompt or retrieval pipeline
Causally fragmented Root cause spans sources, time windows, and entities across siloed stores

How CDF changes the economics

MDL solves the retention and promotion economics; normalized, promoted, and federated access solves the context problem. Organizations land all data affordably, promote mission-relevant slices, and feed AI systems curated evidence—Parquet summaries, vector indexes, SPL aggregations, and governed MCP tool results—rather than repetitive raw events.

1.5M–2M+ tokens per raw security investigation (typical estimate)
3K–15K tokens with curated, promoted, or aggregated context

For responsible AI and data governance

MDL provides governed discoverability and controlled promotion—so AI systems access only approved data slices with audit trails, rather than unconstrained dumps from unmanaged lakes. This supports state executive orders on AI, higher-ed research ethics requirements, and frameworks (CJIS, FERPA, agency-scoped access) that demand lineage and access control before model consumption.
