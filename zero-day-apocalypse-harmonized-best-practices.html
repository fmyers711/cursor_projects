Executive summary

For state agencies and higher education institutions, the core finding is straightforward: patching remains necessary, but it is no longer sufficient. Traditional defense-in-depth—patch management, perimeter firewalls, WAF, EDR, and network segmentation—still matters, but it cannot reliably stop application-layer zero-day exploitation when attackers can reach vulnerable services at machine speed.

Frontier AI models such as Anthropic's Claude Mythos Preview have made this urgency concrete. Vulnerability discovery is no longer a human-speed activity. Near-zero-day windows—sometimes measured in hours, not weeks—are functionally equivalent to zero days for most public-sector and campus environments with legacy systems, complex dependencies, and slow validation cycles.

As a CIO, CISO, CTO, or CDO, your board, legislature, regents, and constituents will ask whether you are prepared for a breach—not whether you have perfect security. The defensible operating model below focuses on controls that leading programs have demonstrated under real-world pressure.

The operating model executive leaders should champion

  • Reduce what attackers can reach — exposure control through Zero Trust and authenticated access
  • Reduce what they can steal — data minimization and lifecycle hygiene (a CDO priority)
  • Prioritize what must be fixed first — contextual, risk-based vulnerability management
  • Strengthen identity — passwordless and strong authentication as a core defensive layer
  • Prepare to respond fast — mature incident response and AI-augmented SecOps when prevention fails

Why Mythos changed the conversation

In April 2026, Anthropic announced Project Glasswing and Claude Mythos Preview—an AI model that autonomously discovered thousands of high-severity, working zero-day vulnerabilities across major operating systems and web browsers. Cisco participated in the Glasswing initiative and validated the implications in its Shields Up guidance.

Mythos did not invent a new category of attack. It reframed the speed variable in risk: discovery and exploit development can now happen in minutes, while patch release and deployment remain human-speed processes. That structural gap is what makes zero-day conversations urgent for executive leadership—not hypothetical fear, but a measurable shift in the vulnerability lifecycle.

What Mythos means for your leadership team

  • CIO / CTO: Budget and architecture decisions must assume patching will lag exploitation. Invest in exposure reduction (ZTNA, authenticated access) and infrastructure hardening—not more patch velocity alone.
  • CISO: Detection and response are now primary survival capabilities, not afterthoughts. Behavioral detection matters when signatures cannot keep pace with unknown exploits.
  • CDO: Data retained beyond business need is breach liability. Minimization is impact reduction when prevention fails.
  • All executives: The question boards and oversight bodies will ask is no longer if you will be tested, but how quickly you detect, contain, and recover.

The new risk equation

Cisco's post-Mythos framing adds speed to the traditional risk model: Risk = likelihood × impact × speed. For state and campus environments—decentralized IT, research systems, citizen and student data, and constrained staff—speed is the factor most likely to overwhelm existing programs.

Three-stage fallback when patching fails

Leading guidance from Cisco's post-Mythos operational framework defines a clear fallback sequence when vulnerability management cannot keep pace:

  1. Vulnerability management — scan, prioritize, patch. Still essential, but no longer the primary line of defense at AI-scale CVE volumes.
  2. Hardening fundamentals — MFA everywhere (especially VPN and admin access), CIS benchmarks and golden images, network segmentation to limit blast radius.
  3. Detection and response — EDR/XDR, behavioral detection, threat hunting, and well-exercised incident response for when exploitation gets through.

Mythos belongs in every zero-day executive briefing because it gives leadership a shared, credible reference point: the threat landscape has structurally changed, and the controls that survived real breaches are the ones worth funding now.

Why state and campus environments are especially exposed

Structural factors CIOs and CISOs cannot ignore

Vulnerabilities are being discovered and exploited faster than most public-sector and higher-education teams can assess, patch, and validate—especially where legacy infrastructure, research systems, decentralized governance, and long data-retention histories intersect.

Why your environment faces elevated risk

  • Legacy and research systems that cannot be patched on aggressive timelines
  • Internet-facing administrative and collaboration platforms (ERP, identity, learning management, file sharing)
  • Large, distributed user populations—students, faculty, contractors, and citizens—with BYOD
  • Sensitive student, citizen, research, and financial data with long retention histories
  • Flat or declining security budgets relative to expanding attack surface and regulatory expectations

The "outrun your neighbor" myth is dead

Security is no longer about being slightly safer than the agency or institution next door. Large-scale threat groups systematically exploit known, reachable vulnerabilities. If a vulnerability exists on a system attackers can reach, assume it will eventually be exploited. Executive leadership must fund exposure reduction and response readiness—not comparative complacency.

What a documented zero-day breach demonstrated

A major Oracle EBS incident (3 TB data breach) showed standard controls failing in sequence: patch management could not address an unknown zero day; WAF and perimeter controls were insufficient because the service had to remain exposed; EDR had limited application-layer visibility; threat intelligence was unavailable because the exploit was not yet known; SSO helped authenticated users but did not protect unauthenticated server-level vulnerabilities; segmentation limited lateral movement but not data already on the compromised host.

Defense-in-depth is necessary—but not sufficient. This is the case study boards and oversight committees understand: layered controls failed individually, and the institution needed a different operating model.

Three-part strategy

The defensible recommendation for executive leadership rests on three principles that work together: reduce exposure, reduce impact, and build resilience.

1. Reduce exposure Make it harder for attackers to reach vulnerable systems—authentication before access, ZTNA, and immediate "tiger team" mitigations.
2. Reduce impact Shrink blast radius through data minimization—remove stale sensitive data before it becomes breach evidence.
3. Build resilience Assume compromise will occur; exercise IR plans and accelerate detection, triage, and response.

1. Reduce exposure — the "moat" strategy

Goal: Buy time when patch windows collapse from weeks to hours.

Proven practices

  • Adopt Zero Trust Network Access (ZTNA). Require users and devices to authenticate before traffic can reach protected systems. ZTNA is positioned as a more granular, better user-experience replacement for traditional VPNs—not merely a strategic architecture discussion, but a practical near-zero-day mitigation.
  • Move critical internet-facing systems behind authentication immediately. For systems that cannot be patched quickly, deploy an emergency "tiger team" operation: place them behind a VPN or load balancer that mandates authentication—even if full ZTNA rollout will take 1–2 years.
  • Prioritize internet-facing systems first. Non-public systems that do not truly need anonymous internet access should not have it.
  • Assume reachability equals exploitability. If attackers can see it, scan it, and reach it, they can exploit it faster than most teams can patch it.
"Zero Trust is no longer just a strategic architecture discussion; it is a practical mitigation for near-zero-day exposure."

2. Reduce impact — data risk minimization

Goal: Assume some compromise will happen. Limit what attackers can exfiltrate when it does.

Best practices

  • Identify stale, unused, or forgotten sensitive data. Old SharePoint repositories, expired research datasets, legacy file shares, and unnecessary exports are common high-risk retention problems in campus and agency environments.
  • Assume a significant share of stored sensitive data has outlived its business purpose—leading programs treat unnecessary retention as liability, not asset.
  • Treat unnecessary sensitive data as a liability, not an asset. Every major incident eventually includes the question: "Why was that file still there?"
  • Build a multi-year data minimization program with policies, ownership, and automation—not a one-time cleanup project.
"Data minimization is breach impact reduction."

3. Build resilience — prepare for inevitable compromise

Goal: Detect faster, investigate faster, recover faster. Resilience matters as much as prevention in the zero-day era.

Best practices

  • Maintain a well-exercised incident response plan—not a document on a shelf, but an operationally mature capability that is tested regularly.
  • Improve detection, triage, and response speed so analysts focus on high-value response work, not repetitive alert triage.
  • Accept that absolute security is impossible. The objective is minimizing likelihood, blast radius, and recovery time—not eliminating all risk.
  • Provide secure, agency- or institution-supported tools to users so they do not seek higher-risk, unvetted alternatives.
"In the zero-day era, resilience matters as much as prevention. You need to know what happened, what was touched, what data was exposed, and what action to take—quickly."

Risk-based vulnerability management

Organizations cannot patch everything fast enough—especially at projected CVE volumes exceeding 59,000 per year. The defensible shift is from "fix everything" to "fix what matters most first," using local context your team already understands.

Contextual prioritization

CVSS severity alone is not enough. Leading programs use contextual scoring models (exemplified by approaches such as Harvard's H-VERS framework) that weigh local environmental factors:

Factor Why it matters
Internet exposure Reachable systems are exploited at scale by automated threat groups
System criticality Mission-critical ERP, identity, research, and citizen-facing services warrant priority
Data sensitivity Systems holding PII, research IP, or regulated data amplify breach impact
Known vulnerability severity Base CVSS or vendor severity as one input—not the only input
Local environmental context Compensating controls, segmentation, authentication requirements, and patch feasibility

Operational outcome

Contextual scoring lets teams focus resources on the riskiest ~5% of vulnerabilities first—internet-facing, mission-critical systems holding sensitive data—instead of treating every finding equally.

How to operationalize prioritization

  • Correlate vulnerability scan data with asset inventory, network exposure, identity activity, and application ownership
  • Build dashboards that show exposure, patch status, critical assets, and trending risk—not just raw CVE counts
  • Empower internal teams with data analytics platforms rather than relying solely on generic consulting engagements for visibility

Identity and authentication

Identity is a critical defensive layer—often the difference between an attempted exploit and a successful breach path. Cisco's post-Mythos guidance identifies MFA as the control that most reliably stops credential-based initial access.

Passwordless authentication

  • Move aggressively toward passwordless authentication. Leading programs report highly effective results against credential phishing—the most common first step in adversary playbooks.
  • Require strong authentication before granting access to sensitive systems. If authentication becomes the moat, weak authentication becomes the bridge for attackers.
  • Integrate identity telemetry into security analytics. Authentication events, anomalous access patterns, and privilege changes should feed detection and investigation workflows—not live in a separate silo.

Why this matters for zero-day readiness

Gaining VPN or credential access is typically the first step in most adversary playbooks. Passwordless is not just a convenience initiative—it is a zero-day readiness control that reduces the probability that exposure reduction measures can be bypassed through stolen credentials.

Lessons from the case study

The Oracle EBS zero-day breach is a well-documented anchor example. It illustrates why layered controls failed individually and why executive leadership must champion a new operating model—not incremental tool purchases.

Implication for your executive team

Security controls that worked yesterday may not stop application-layer zero-day exploitation tomorrow. CIOs and CISOs need layered visibility across application, identity, endpoint, network, vulnerability, and data activity—with the ability to correlate signals and respond quickly when any single control fails.

Culture, governance, and supply chain

Zero-day readiness requires a governance shift that executive leadership must sponsor—not delegate entirely to security operations.

What CIOs and CISOs must drive culturally

  • Security is operating discipline, not a tooling problem. Leadership must treat exposure reduction, data hygiene, and response speed as funded, ongoing programs with measurable outcomes.
  • Absolute security is impossible. Adopt informed risk acceptance: minimize risk, provide sanctioned secure tools, and steer users away from high-risk alternatives.
  • Empower internal talent. Many state and campus organizations already have skilled staff who can build dashboards, analytics, and AI-driven workflows—when given the right data platform and executive air cover.

Address Shadow IT and supply chain risk

  • AI is enabling rapid "vibe-coded" applications and open-source agent tooling—creating an explosion of ungoverned Shadow IT.
  • Custom-built internal tools must be maintained with the same rigor as enterprise-grade software: patching, access control, logging, and lifecycle management.
  • Monitor and govern both internal AI models and unvetted external tools (including open-source AI agents) with guardrails appropriate to your risk tolerance.

AI for security operations

AI is not only an adversary force multiplier—it is a defensive accelerator when applied to security operations with appropriate governance. This is the same dual-use reality Mythos surfaced: the capability exists on both sides of the conflict.

Automate initial alert investigation

Leading campus programs have built internal AI tooling that reduced initial security alert investigation time from hours to under a minute—freeing analysts for incident response. The defensible outcome is analyst time reallocated to containment, not tool novelty.

Best practices for AI-augmented SecOps

  • Use AI to handle repetitive triage: enrich alerts, correlate context, and produce initial investigation summaries
  • Keep human analysts in the loop for validation, escalation, and response decisions
  • Apply the same governance rigor to AI-built security tools as to any production system
  • Measure outcomes: time-to-triage, analyst hours saved, and mean time to contain—not just tool deployment

Unified visibility enables AI and humans alike

Whether investigations are manual or AI-assisted, teams need unified telemetry across endpoint, identity, network, cloud, application, vulnerability, and asset data. Fragmented visibility slows both human analysts and AI agents—and increases the risk of missed correlations during fast-moving zero-day events.

Navigating the zero-day era: A Cisco + Splunk solution framework

A proven strategic frame

Leading higher-education programs responding to the post-Mythos landscape share a clear thesis: patching alone is insufficient when exploitation can outpace deployment. Their model shifts defense from comparative safety to reduce exposure, prioritize ruthlessly, and prepare for inevitable compromise.

Five operating principles validated in production environments:

  • Reduce exposure — minimize internet-facing infrastructure (ZTNA before VPN)
  • Strengthen authentication — go passwordless to eliminate credential-based entry
  • Prioritize by local risk context — fix the riskiest ~5% first
  • Minimize data liability — dispose of sensitive data that no longer serves a business purpose
  • Prepare for compromise — well-exercised IR plus AI-accelerated investigation

Recommended 90-day action plan

The following checklist distills the highest-confidence recommendations into an actionable starting point for state government and higher-education executive teams.

Immediate (0–30 days)

  • Inventory internet-facing systems that hold sensitive data or support critical operations
  • Launch a "tiger team" to move highest-risk exposed systems behind VPN or authenticated load balancers
  • Define a contextual vulnerability scoring model (exposure + criticality + data sensitivity + severity)
  • Identify top 5% of vulnerabilities for immediate remediation focus
  • Validate incident response plan readiness with a tabletop exercise focused on zero-day scenarios

Near-term (30–60 days)

  • Begin ZTNA pilot for highest-risk application tiers
  • Launch passwordless authentication rollout for privileged and remote-access users
  • Start data minimization assessment: stale SharePoint, research archives, legacy exports
  • Build executive and operational dashboards for exposure, patch status, and critical assets
  • Establish governance for internal AI tools and Shadow IT applications

Strategic (60–90 days and beyond)

  • Scale ZTNA program with phased rollout plan (accept 1–2 year full deployment timeline)
  • Operationalize multi-year data lifecycle and retention automation
  • Deploy AI-augmented alert triage for repetitive SecOps workflows
  • Integrate identity, vulnerability, network, and application telemetry for unified investigation
  • Provide secure, institution-supported collaboration and AI tools to reduce risky user workarounds

Bottom line

The practical operating model for surviving the zero-day era

  1. Reduce what attackers can reach. ZTNA, authenticated access, and immediate exposure controls buy time when patch windows collapse.
  2. Reduce what they can steal. Data minimization shrinks blast radius when prevention fails.
  3. Prioritize what must be fixed first. Contextual vulnerability scoring focuses scarce resources on the riskiest ~5%.
  4. Strengthen identity. Passwordless authentication eliminates the most common initial access path.
  5. Prepare to respond fast. Mature incident response and AI-augmented investigation are now core survival capabilities—not optional enhancements.

This is not a call to abandon defense-in-depth. It is a call to evolve it for the post-Mythos era: complement traditional controls with exposure reduction, impact minimization, contextual prioritization, stronger identity, and faster response. For state agencies and campuses, the oversight question is not if your controls will be tested, but how quickly you can detect, contain, and recover when they are.
