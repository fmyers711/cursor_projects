1. Cost Perspective

Potential benefits

Google SecOps licensing may reduce annual platform costs depending on:

  • Google Cloud commitments
  • Existing Chronicle licensing
  • Existing Google ecosystem discounts
  • Included threat intelligence
  • Native Gemini AI capabilities

Software licensing alone may appear attractive. However, software licensing represents only a portion of Total Cost of Ownership (TCO). For organizations similar to FTB, the larger costs are often associated with migration, validation, and operational disruption.

Hidden costs

Organizations frequently underestimate:

  • Thousands of engineering hours
  • Detection validation
  • False positive tuning
  • Incident response workflow changes
  • Audit evidence recreation
  • Compliance reporting updates

These costs frequently exceed first-year licensing savings.

Cost observation — optimize before migrating

If Splunk licensing can instead be optimized using:

  • Workload Pricing
  • Entity Pricing
  • Architecture optimization
  • Data lifecycle optimization

then much of the expected financial benefit may be achieved without incurring migration costs.

Cost risk rating: 🔴 High License savings alone rarely justify the full TCO of a SIEM replacement for a mature deployment.

2. Operational Risk Perspective

Operational risk is the largest concern. A SIEM migration affects every stage of the SOC lifecycle.

Current workflow

Data Sources → Splunk → Normalization → Correlation Searches → Risk-Based Alerting → SOC Investigation → Response → Metrics

Replacing the SIEM affects every box.

Operational risks

Detection regression

Every custom analytic must be rebuilt, including SPL searches, RBA scoring, threat models, correlation logic, and dashboards. Even if migrated successfully, behavior often changes.

Analyst productivity

Analysts know Splunk search language, dashboards, investigation workflows, and existing playbooks. Switching platforms typically causes slower investigations, longer onboarding, and reduced productivity for months.

MTTR risk

  • ↑ Mean Time to Detect
  • ↑ Mean Time to Respond
  • ↓ Analyst efficiency
  • ↓ Operational confidence

Governance risk

During migration, two SIEMs may run simultaneously — creating duplicate investigations, inconsistent alerting, duplicate tuning, and reporting discrepancies.

Change fatigue

FTB is simultaneously considering cloud modernization, FedRAMP initiatives, cost optimization, and architecture modernization. Adding a SIEM replacement compounds organizational risk.

Operational risk rating: 🔴 Very High

3. Loss of Capability Perspective

This is where leadership should perform the deepest due diligence. Google SecOps is a capable cloud-native SIEM; however, capability parity is not guaranteed.

A. Risk-Based Alerting (RBA)

Splunk ES has mature RBA capabilities: multiple risk objects, weighted scoring, adaptive thresholds, accumulated risk, and threat object correlation. Google SecOps has entity-based detections and risk scoring, but organizations frequently need to redesign mature Splunk RBA implementations rather than migrate them directly.

Risk: 🔴 Medium–High

B. Detection content

FTB's current Splunk environment likely contains years of tuning, custom SPL, fraud detections, insider threat logic, correlation searches, and MITRE mappings — representing institutional knowledge requiring rewrite, validation, and retesting.

Risk: 🔴 High

C. Investigation experience

Splunk ES provides Event Timeline, Risk Timeline, asset context, identity context, drilldowns, and Enterprise Security dashboards. Equivalent workflows in Google SecOps often require redesign.

Risk: 🟡 Medium

D. Data integrations

Current integrations appear to include CrowdStrike, Zscaler, Microsoft, Windows, AWS, fraud systems, and infrastructure telemetry. Migration requires parser validation, field normalization, and schema mapping.

Risk: 🔴 High

E. Splunk ecosystem

Current or future investments may include SOAR, ITSI, Observability, Asset Risk Intelligence (ARI), and Mission Control. If those are strategic initiatives, Google SecOps reduces platform consolidation opportunities.

For example, Splunk Asset Risk Intelligence can consolidate portions of several third-party asset risk management capabilities (such as Axonius, Kenna, Panaseer, Rapid7, Qualys, and others), though specialized scanning and remediation functions typically remain external.

Maturity Impact

The current technology maturity provides a stable foundation for data normalization and visibility, which is difficult and time-consuming to rebuild.

Technology maturity

The gaps in Proactive Response (25%) and Unified Workflows (21%) represent specific functional deficiencies rather than a failure of the entire architecture. A "start over" strategy would necessitate re-tuning, re-normalizing data, and re-training personnel, leading to significant operational downtime.

Process maturity (55% overall)

  • Foundational Visibility (77%) — Strong asset, identity, and data governance visibility.
  • Proactive Response (73%) — Effective incident handling with established templates and formal risk management frameworks.
  • Guided Insights (46%) — Basic MTTR/MTTD tracking exists; opportunity to better leverage insights for tactical decisions.
  • Unified Workflows (25%) — Siloed processes prevent fully automated, cross-functional security operations.

Overall, this capability level provides a reliable reactive and semi-proactive posture, but limits the organization's ability to scale through seamless, automated, and continuous improvement workflows.

Impact of starting over

  • Loss of years of custom use cases, alert history, and tuned detection logic
  • Loss of established data normalization (CIM/OCSF), creating a massive blind spot during transition
  • Significant productivity loss while learning new interfaces and workflows
  • Financial resources diverted toward procurement and implementation rather than optimizing current security gaps
  • Degradation of "People" maturity — existing certifications and role-based training are often vendor-specific

Conclusion on maturity

  • Prioritize maturing the current technology stack by targeting gaps in Proactive Response and Unified Workflows
  • Investing in automation and orchestration on top of the existing foundation is more cost-effective and less risky than a complete technology replacement
  • Primary focus: bridge the gap between Guided Insights and Proactive Response to maximize ROI of existing infrastructure

Executive Recommendation

Before approving a migration, leadership should require objective evidence that Google SecOps can achieve functional parity for FTB's highest-value use cases without introducing unacceptable operational risk.

Specifically, the decision should be based on:

  1. A side-by-side capability assessment covering custom detections, Risk-Based Alerting, fraud monitoring, investigative workflows, and all critical integrations.
  2. A comprehensive Total Cost of Ownership (TCO) analysis that includes software licensing, engineering effort, professional services, retraining, dual-platform operations, validation, and change management — not just subscription costs.
  3. An optimization review of the existing Splunk deployment, including evaluation of Workload, Entity, and Ingest pricing models, architectural rationalization, and opportunities to modernize existing workflows before pursuing a platform replacement.

Recommended near-term strategy: Optimize and modernize the existing Splunk deployment before committing to a full SIEM replacement.
