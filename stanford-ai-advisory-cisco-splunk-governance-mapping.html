Mapping overview

Stanford committee posture

The AI at Stanford Advisory Committee (March–June 2024) recommended flexible guardrails over rigid policy, human-centered AI that augments rather than replaces people, and resistance to "AI exceptionalism"—existing laws, privacy rules, and professional standards still apply. The committee identified policy gaps across administration, education, and research while encouraging experimentation through sandboxes, equitable LLM access, and regularly updated guidance.

Stanford guiding principles → Cisco + Splunk mapping

The committee adapted Cornell's human-centeredness framework into seven guiding principles. Below, each principle is mapped to specific capabilities from the SLED AI governance stack.

1. Human Oversight

Strong

Stanford: Clear authority for procurement, maintenance, monitoring, sunsetting; contingency plans for high-risk AI; re-engage human control when systems fail.

Mapping:

  • Astrix Identity Graph — maps each agent/NHI to a human owner (GOVERN 2.1)
  • Three-layer inventory — Splunk ITSI + AI Defense AI Cloud Visibility + Astrix (GOVERN 1.6)
  • Splunk SOAR — playbooks for decommissioning, credential revocation, human approval gates (GOVERN 1.7, MANAGE 2.4)
  • AI Defense — runtime disengagement of misbehaving agent chains

2. Human Alignment

Partial

Stanford: AI supports mission and values; stakeholder consultation; documented scope, capabilities, and limitations evaluated regularly.

Mapping:

  • Splunk AI Observability (Galileo) — traces intended use and task boundaries (MAP 1.1, MAP 2.1–2.3)
  • AI Defense — AI-traffic flow mapping; shadow AI surfacing (MAP 1.1)
  • Gap: Stakeholder consultation and mission-alignment review are institutional processes; technology supplies documentation and monitoring evidence

3. Human Professionalism

Policy-led

Stanford: Community members exercise judgment; responsible for work quality and veracity regardless of AI assistance; abide by laws and professional norms.

Mapping:

  • Splunk AI Observability (Galileo) — hallucination detection supports author verification
  • AI Defense validation — red-teaming surfaces quality risks before deployment
  • Gap: Professional responsibility and Honor Code adherence are policy/education matters; technology provides detection and disclosure support, not judgment

4. Ethical and Safe Use

Strong

Stanford: Understand implications before deployment; promote justice and autonomy; free from bias and unlawful discrimination; decommission unsafe systems.

Mapping:

  • Splunk AI Observability (Galileo) — bias/fairness metrics, safety evaluation (MEASURE 2.11, 2.6)
  • AI Defense — 50+ runtime safety categories; supply-chain scanning (GOVERN 6.1, MANAGE 3.1)
  • Splunk SOAR + AI Defense — decommissioning and blocklist workflows (GOVERN 1.7, MANAGE 2.4)

5. Privacy, Security & Confidentiality

Full

Stanford: Assess legality of personal data use; transparency in AI decisions; no confidential/privileged data to generative AI tools.

Mapping:

  • AI Defense — PII/PCI/PHI leakage prevention; runtime data guardrails (MEASURE 2.10)
  • Cisco segmentation + Hypershield — controlled inference environments for FERPA/CJIS
  • Splunk ES/SOAR — detect and respond to policy violations; audit trails
  • In-environment inference — metadata only to Cisco; supports data-residency requirements

6. Data Quality and Control

Strong

Stanford: Legal/ethical data collection; documented provenance; tested systems with documented risks; controls against bad actors.

Mapping:

  • AI Defense Supply Chain Risk Management — scans models, datasets, MCP repositories (GOVERN 6.1, MAP 4.1)
  • Splunk AI Observability (Galileo) — production drift and integrity monitoring (MEASURE 2.4)
  • Cisco Secure Workload + WideField/Astrix — segmentation and identity controls against exploitation

7. AI Golden Rule

Partial

Stanford: "Use or share AI outputs as you would have others use or share AI output with you"; consider role reversal and transparent disclosure.

Mapping:

  • Splunk AI Observability (Galileo) — tracing and explainability support disclosure (MEASURE 2.8–2.9)
  • AI Defense validation reporting — documents how systems were tested
  • Gap: Norm-setting and community standards are cultural; technology enables transparency and audit, not ethical judgment

Gaps, complementary investments & implementation priorities

Where institutional policy must lead

Stanford correctly notes that flexible guidelines often suffice over rigid policy. The Cisco + Splunk stack operationalizes technical guardrails but cannot replace:

  • Honor Code and academic integrity policy revision (Board on Conduct Affairs)
  • Human professionalism and AI Golden Rule cultural norms
  • Workforce "augment not replace" vision and change management (UHR)
  • IRB and SDOC governance process design for research data
  • Central guidelines website content and AI literacy curricula (UIT, CTL, GSE)
  • Research computing / HPC capacity expansion

Executive summary for higher-ed leadership

Alignment conclusion

Stanford's advisory committee and the Cisco + Splunk SLED AI governance framework share the same foundational premise: AI adoption requires guardrails that enable experimentation while honoring human-centered values, existing law, and accountable oversight. The committee's emphasis on inventory, bias assessment, confidential-data protection, equitable access, procurement review, and audit-ready evidence maps directly to the technical capabilities already articulated in the governance document.

Recommended narrative for Stanford-style institutions

  • Policy sets intent; Stanford's principles and flexible guidelines define what the community should do.
  • Technology proves control; Cisco + Splunk supplies the inventory, evaluation, runtime protection, and audit evidence that make guidelines enforceable at campus scale.
  • Build on existing investments; Institutions already running Splunk ES/SOAR and Cisco Duo/ISE/Firewall extend those platforms rather than standing up parallel AI governance tools.
  • Close the highest-risk gaps first: confidential data to external GenAI, unvetted procurement, and high-impact decisions (admissions, hiring, grading) without documented bias assessment.
  • Complement with culture: sandboxes, CTL workshops, Honor Code updates, and the AI Golden Rule remain essential—and are strengthened when backed by telemetry, not replaced by it.

NIST AI RMF structures the compliance program; MITRE ATLAS hardens against adversarial techniques; Stanford's advisory committee defines the institutional values and policy priorities. Together, they form a complete higher-education AI governance model—principles, policies, and provable controls.
